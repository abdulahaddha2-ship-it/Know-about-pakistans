The Institution

Pakistan's Bedrock Defence Force

The Pakistan Army is the land-based branch of the Pakistan Armed Forces. It is the world's sixth-largest army by active military personnel, with over 650,000 active soldiers and more than 500,000 reserve personnel as of 2026.

Founded on 14 August 1947 simultaneously with the independence of Pakistan, the army inherited approximately one-third of the British Indian Army's assets, regiments, and manpower. British officers served in senior positions through the transition period, with General Sir Frank Messervy serving as the first Commander-in-Chief.

The army is headquartered at General Headquarters (GHQ) in Rawalpindi, Punjab. The Supreme Commander of the Pakistan Army is constitutionally the President of Pakistan, while operational command rests with the Chief of Army Staff (COAS), currently a four-star General.

The army's motto — "Iman, Taqwa, Jihad fi Sabilillah" (Faith, Piety, Holy War in the Path of Allah) — reflects the spiritual and professional values that define the institution.

The Beginning

14 August 1947

When the British Raj ended on the midnight of 14 August 1947, two nations were born from the crucible of Partition. Amidst the largest human migration in history — 10 to 20 million people displaced, over one million killed — the Pakistan Army came into existence from nothing. It was not handed an army; it had to build one while simultaneously fighting its first war.

The division of the British Indian Army was brutal and hasty. Pakistan received approximately 30% of the assets — six armoured regiments, eight artillery regiments, and roughly 140,000 troops. Much of the heavy equipment, ammunition, and supplies was delayed or outright denied. Senior officers, largely British, were serving on borrowed time. Regiments were split overnight along religious lines, with Muslim soldiers reporting to Pakistan and Hindu/Sikh soldiers to India.

"Pakistan comes into being in the most difficult circumstances. The army is our first shield. It must be ready to defend every inch of this new nation."

— Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah, August 1947

Within weeks of independence, Pakistani and Indian forces clashed in Kashmir. Captain Muhammad Sarwar of the Punjab Regiment became the first Pakistani soldier to earn the Nishan-e-Haider — and the first to die in the service of the new nation. The army was already being forged in fire before it had even fully formed its own identity.

Historical Record

1947 to 2026

Seven decades of service, war, sacrifice, and evolution. Every chapter written in the blood of those who answered the call.

14 Aug 1947Foundation

Pakistan Army Founded

Created from division of British Indian Army. Initial strength ~150,000 troops with British officers in senior positions. General Sir Frank Messervy appointed first Commander-in-Chief. Army inherits about 30% of British Indian Army assets amidst the chaos of Partition.

Oct 1947 – Jan 1949War

First Kashmir War (1947–48)

Pakistan Army's baptism by fire. Deployed to support Kashmiri fighters against Indian forces. Captain Muhammad Sarwar earns — and dies for — the first Nishan-e-Haider. UN-brokered ceasefire in January 1949 establishes the Line of Control that exists to this day.

1951Milestone

First Pakistani Commander-in-Chief

General Ayub Khan becomes the first Pakistani-born Commander-in-Chief, ending British command of the army. A historic transition marking the full indigenisation of the officer corps.

1954Alliance

SEATO & CENTO Membership

Pakistan joins the Southeast Asia Treaty Organization and later the Central Treaty Organization (Baghdad Pact), aligning with the Western bloc during the Cold War. U.S. military aid begins flowing to modernize the army's equipment and training.

1956Elite

SSG Founded — Special Services Group

Pakistan establishes its elite special operations force modeled partly on the U.S. Special Forces. The SSG — known as the "Black Storks" for their distinctive berets — would go on to conduct some of Pakistan's most sensitive military operations across seven decades.

Sep 1965War

Second Indo-Pakistan War — The 17-Day War

Operation Grand Slam attempts to cut off Indian supply lines to Kashmir. The Battle of Lahore and the Battle of Chawinda — one of the largest tank engagements since WWII — define this conflict. Pakistan deploys Patton tanks against Indian Centurions. Despite tactical successes, UN ceasefire ends the war. Two Nishan-e-Haider awarded posthumously. Indian forces halted at the outskirts of Lahore.

1971War

Bangladesh Liberation War — The Great Tragedy

The darkest chapter. East Pakistan separates to become Bangladesh following Indian military intervention and civil war. The Pakistan Army fights on two fronts — both East and West — and suffers its greatest defeat. 93,000 Pakistani troops surrender in Dhaka on 16 December 1971. Three Nishan-e-Haider awarded. The army undergoes sweeping reforms under Z.A. Bhutto's government, including reduction in political power.

1974Nuclear

Nuclear Programme Begins

Following India's 1974 nuclear test, Pakistan accelerates its own nuclear weapons programme. The army's Strategic Plans Division is later established to manage the nuclear arsenal, delivery systems, and command structure under the National Command Authority.

1979–1989Cold War

Afghan-Soviet War — ISI & Mujahideen

Pakistan Army and ISI coordinate with the CIA to arm and train Afghan Mujahideen fighting Soviet forces in Afghanistan. The Peshawar-based operation becomes one of the largest covert military operations in Cold War history. Pakistan becomes a frontline state, receiving billions in U.S. military aid. The war fundamentally reshapes the region's geopolitics and seeds future insurgency problems.

1984–PresentSiachen

Siachen Conflict — World's Highest Battlefield

India's Operation Meghdoot in April 1984 seizes positions on the Siachen Glacier before Pakistan can act. Pakistan Army deploys Northern Light Infantry and SSG to establish and hold posts at altitudes above 6,000m. Temperatures drop to -60°C. More casualties from weather than combat. The 2012 Gayari avalanche kills 138 Pakistani soldiers in a single moment. The conflict continues with no formal resolution.

1989–2005Peacekeeping

UN Peacekeeping Era — Global Deployment

Pakistan dramatically expands UN peacekeeping contributions. Deployments to Somalia (UNOSOM), Rwanda, Bosnia (UNPROFOR), Sierra Leone, Liberia, Sudan, Haiti, and Congo. By 2000, Pakistan becomes the world's largest troop-contributing nation to UN peacekeeping missions — a distinction it holds for over a decade.

May 1998Nuclear

Nuclear Tests — Chagai

Pakistan conducts five nuclear tests at Chagai in Balochistan on 28 May 1998, in response to India's Pokhran-II tests. A sixth test is conducted on 30 May. Pakistan becomes the world's seventh declared nuclear state. The Strategic Plans Division assumes formal responsibility for nuclear assets and doctrine.

May–Jul 1999War

Kargil War — Operation Badr

Pakistani regular forces and Mujahideen infiltrate positions overlooking the Kargil-Leh highway in Indian-administered Kashmir during winter. Intense fighting at altitudes of 5,000m+. India launches massive counter-offensive using air power. International pressure — including from the U.S. — leads to Pakistani withdrawal after approximately 11 weeks. The conflict brings two nuclear states to the brink of full war.

2001Turning Point

Post-9/11 Pivot — War on Terror

Following 9/11, Pakistan under General Pervez Musharraf reverses its Taliban-support policy and joins the U.S.-led coalition. The army pivots from an India-centric conventional posture to counter-insurgency in the tribal belt. A decade of internal conflict begins as former Afghan jihad allies become Pakistan's enemies.

2004–2008Operation

Operations in FATA — Waziristan

Operation Zalzala in South Waziristan; operations in the Bajaur, Mohmand, and Khyber agencies. The army suffers significant losses in unfamiliar counter-insurgency terrain. Suicide bombings begin targeting military installations, mosques, and markets across Pakistan.

Oct 2005Disaster

Kashmir Earthquake — 87,000 Dead

A 7.6 magnitude earthquake devastates AJK and KPK. 87,000 killed, 3.5 million homeless as winter approaches. The Pakistan Army becomes the primary relief and rescue force, deploying 100,000+ troops, engineers, and medics. A massive international relief operation is coordinated through army logistics. The army's performance earns international praise.

2009Operation

Operation Rah-e-Rast — Reclaiming Swat

Pakistan's largest military operation at that time. 30,000+ troops deployed to clear the Swat Valley of TTP (Tehrik-e-Taliban Pakistan). 2 million civilians displaced as army systematically retakes every village and mountain post. By September 2009, civilian administration restored. A landmark in Pakistan's counter-insurgency capability.

2010Disaster

Catastrophic Floods — 20 Million Affected

The worst floods in Pakistan's recorded history inundate one-fifth of the country. Pakistan Army deploys 170,000 troops and rescues over 500,000 people in the largest civil-military relief operation in the country's history. Army engineers build 278 bridges and restore 8,000km of roads.

Jun 2014Operation

Operation Zarb-e-Azb — The Decisive Strike

Pakistan's largest ever military offensive. 30,000+ troops enter North Waziristan — Pakistan's most lawless tribal region and a sanctuary for TTP, Haqqani Network, Al-Qaeda, IMU, and affiliated groups. Hundreds of militant tunnels, weapon caches, and command centres destroyed. The operation clears the entire tribal region and kills over 3,400 militants, though many escape into Afghanistan. One of the most significant counter-insurgency successes in Pakistan's history.

16 Dec 2014Tragedy

APS Peshawar Massacre — 132 Children Martyred

TTP militants attack Army Public School Peshawar, killing 132 schoolchildren and 17 staff in the deadliest terrorist attack in Pakistan's history. The nation mourns in unity. Army reacts by intensifying operations, launching the National Action Plan, and abolishing the moratorium on the death penalty for terrorism. The APS attack becomes the turning point that galvanises public and political support for a full military campaign against the Taliban.

2015Formation

Special Security Division — CPEC Protection

A dedicated military formation of 15,000+ troops established to guard China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) projects, workers, and infrastructure across all four provinces. As CPEC investment reaches $65+ billion, the SSD becomes one of the army's most critical new roles.

2017–PresentOperation

Operation Radd-ul-Fasaad — Eliminating the Remnants

Nationwide counter-terrorism operation targeting sleeper cells, weapons caches, and terrorist networks. Conducted in collaboration with intelligence agencies, law enforcement, and paramilitary forces. By 2024, over 58,000 intelligence-based operations conducted across Pakistan, drastically reducing major attacks.

2022Disaster

Super Floods 2022 — One-Third of Pakistan Submerged

Unprecedented monsoon floods affect 33 million people and submerge one-third of Pakistan. The army deploys in the largest civil emergency response in its history — 177,000 troops, 50+ helicopters, and 1,200 boats rescue over 900,000 stranded civilians. Army engineers work continuously for months rebuilding infrastructure.

2022–PresentOperation

Operation Azm-e-Istehkam — Renewed Resolve

Large-scale counter-insurgency operations in KPK and Balochistan targeting resurgent TTP elements following the 2021 U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan. Simultaneous focus on CPEC security, border fencing with Afghanistan (over 2,600km of fence completed), and economic zone protection. Operations continue as of April 2026.

2024–2026Current

Present Day — Multi-Front Engagement

As of April 2026, the Pakistan Army is simultaneously managing counter-terrorism in KPK and Balochistan under Operation Azm-e-Istehkam, maintaining LoC vigilance against India, guarding CPEC infrastructure, participating in 4 active UN peacekeeping missions, and conducting humanitarian operations following flash floods in Northern Pakistan (early 2026). Modernisation continues with induction of new armoured vehicles, drones, and air defence systems.

Pakistan's Highest Honour

Nishan-e-Haider ☪

The Nishan-e-Haider (نشان حیدر) — "Sign of Haider Ali" — is Pakistan's highest and most sacred military award. Equivalent to the British Victoria Cross or the American Medal of Honor, it has been awarded only six times since Pakistan's founding in 1947. Every recipient died earning it. Every one was awarded posthumously. Their names are inscribed at GHQ Rawalpindi, and their regiments carry the honour for eternity.
MS
Captain Muhammad Sarwar Shaheed
Punjab Regiment · 1948

First recipient and first martyr. During the 1947–48 Kashmir War at Tithwal, he single-handedly attacked and destroyed enemy bunkers under heavy fire. He cut the fuse wire of a booby trap to allow his men to advance, sacrificing his life in the act. Martyred 27 July 1948, age 35.

Elite & Special Units

Special Forces & Specialized Arms

Tier 1 Elite

Special Services Group (SSG)

Pakistan's premier special operations force, founded 1956. Specializes in counter-terrorism, direct action, HALO/HAHO parachute insertion, hostage rescue, and unconventional warfare. The 18-month selection course has under 5% pass rate. Known as the "Black Storks." Deployed in every major operation since Swat.

Mountain Elite

Northern Light Infantry (NLI)

A specialized mountain warfare regiment recruited exclusively from Gilgit-Baltistan. World experts in high-altitude combat, glacier warfare, and arctic conditions. Primary defenders of the Siachen region at 6,000m+. Converted from scouts to a regular regiment in 1999 — earning the conversion in blood at Kargil.

Aviation

Army Aviation Corps

Operates AH-1Z Viper attack helicopters, Mi-17 transport helicopters, and a growing fleet of UAV/drone systems including Chinese-supplied WZ-10 armed drones. Provides close air support, reconnaissance, MEDEVAC, and troop transport across all theatres. Critical in Swat and North Waziristan operations.

Nuclear

Strategic Plans Division (SPD)

Manages Pakistan's nuclear arsenal, delivery systems, and the nuclear command-and-control structure under the National Command Authority. Responsible for the operational readiness of land-based ballistic missiles (Ghaznavi, Shaheen series), sea-based deterrent, and nuclear doctrine. An army two-star heads the division.

CPEC Security

Special Security Division (SSD)

A dedicated formation of 15,000+ troops established 2015 to protect Chinese CPEC workers, engineers, and infrastructure. Organized into 9 battalions guarding projects from Gwadar Port to the KKH in Gilgit-Baltistan. As CPEC investment has grown beyond $65 billion, the SSD's role has expanded proportionally.

Intelligence

Directorate of Military Intelligence (MI)

The army's internal intelligence arm responsible for counterintelligence, field security, operational intelligence, and threat assessment at formation level. Works in coordination with ISI on national security matters and conducts intelligence-based operations in all active theatres.

Honours System

Military Awards & Decorations

Pakistan's system of military honours recognises acts from supreme battlefield gallantry to distinguished careers of service. In order of precedence:

🏅

Nishan-e-Haider (نشان حیدر)

Grade 1 — Highest Military Honour

Pakistan's supreme military award, equivalent to the Victoria Cross. Awarded only 6 times since 1947, all posthumously. Recipients' names are inscribed at GHQ Rawalpindi permanently.

🎖

Hilal-e-Jurat (ہلال جرأت)

Grade 2 — Crescent of Courage

Second highest gallantry award. Given for conspicuous acts of bravery in the face of the enemy. A bar with rosette may be awarded for a second act of equal distinction.

⭐

Sitara-e-Jurat (ستارہ جرأت)

Grade 3 — Star of Courage

Awarded for individual acts of conspicuous bravery and leadership under fire. Frequently awarded to junior officers and NCOs who display exceptional courage in close combat.

🏵

Tamgha-e-Jurat (تمغہ جرأت)

Grade 4 — Medal of Courage

For acts of bravery that distinguish the recipient from peers. Frequently awarded to soldiers in counter-insurgency and anti-terror operations on the front lines.

🎗

Sitara-e-Basalat (ستارہ بسالت)

Meritorious — Star of Distinction

Awarded for distinguished service over a period, or for a single act of exceptional merit. Given to officers who demonstrate exemplary leadership and professionalism beyond normal duty.

🏆

Tamgha-e-Basalat (تمغہ بسالت)

Meritorious — Distinguished Service

Pakistan's most widely awarded gallantry medal. Available to all ranks from Sepoy upwards. Given for individual acts of bravery or meritorious service in operational areas.

Campaign & Service Medals
UN Service

United Nations Service Medal

Awarded to personnel who serve 90+ days in UN peacekeeping missions. Pakistan has earned this medal across 45+ missions in 30+ countries, logging over 200,000 cumulative troop-months of UN service.

Zarb-e-Azb

Zarb-e-Azb Campaign Medal

Awarded to all personnel who participated in Operation Zarb-e-Azb (2014–2016) in North Waziristan — Pakistan's largest military operation that cleared thousands of km² of militant territory.

Siachen

Siachen Glacier Service Medal

Awarded to soldiers who serve at posts on the Siachen Glacier above 5,000m. Considered one of the most physically demanding postings of any military in the world, with temperatures reaching -60°C.

Global Service

UN Peacekeeping — World's Largest Contributor

Pakistan has been one of the world's largest contributors to United Nations peacekeeping operations for over three decades. The army has deployed troops, military observers, police, and staff officers to over 45 missions across 30+ countries on five continents — from the jungles of Congo to the deserts of Darfur, from the streets of Beirut to the mountains of Sierra Leone.

Pakistani peacekeepers have served in Somalia, Bosnia, Rwanda, Sierra Leone, Liberia, Haiti, Sudan, South Sudan, Mali, Central African Republic, Côte d'Ivoire, Lebanon (UNIFIL), Cyprus, and more. The army collectively has logged over 200,000 troop-months of UN service — more than any other nation in history.

Leadership & Command

Chiefs of Army Staff have defined the army's character across seventy-eight years. From British-led transition to the nuclear era.

AK
General Ayub Khan
1st Pakistani COAS

First Pakistani-born Commander-in-Chief (1951–58). Later became President of Pakistan. Modernized the army during the Cold War era with U.S. assistance. Led Pakistan through SEATO and CENTO alliances.

Term: 1951–1958
YK
General Yahya Khan
COAS & President

Led Pakistan during the 1971 war and the catastrophic loss of East Pakistan. His tenure ended in the military's worst defeat. The tragedy led to sweeping civilianisation reforms under Z.A. Bhutto.

Term: 1966–1971
ZU
General Zia-ul-Haq
COAS & President

Longest-serving military ruler (1977–88). Coordinated the anti-Soviet Afghan jihad with the CIA. Oversaw the army's Islamisation. Died in a plane crash in August 1988 under mysterious circumstances.

Term: 1976–1988
PM
General Pervez Musharraf
COAS & President

Orchestrated the Kargil operation, then seized power in 1999 coup. Post-9/11, reversed Pakistan's Afghan policy and joined the War on Terror. Resigned in 2008 under political pressure. Died in exile 2023.

Term: 1998–2007
RB
General Raheel Sharif
COAS — Zarb-e-Azb Era

Launched Operation Zarb-e-Azb in 2014 — the army's largest offensive. Directed the response to the APS massacre and the National Action Plan. Retired in 2016; led the Saudi-led Islamic Military Counter Terrorism Coalition.

Term: 2013–2016
AM
General Asim Munir
Current COAS (2026)

Appointed COAS in November 2022. Has overseen Operation Azm-e-Istehkam, accelerated Afghanistan border fencing, and continued CPEC security expansion. Manages ongoing counterterrorism in KPK and Balochistan as of April 2026.

Term: 2022–Present

Terrain & Deployment

Pakistan's Strategic Geography

The army operates across the most varied and extreme terrain of any military in the world — from -60°C glaciers to 50°C deserts.

🏔

Siachen Glacier

World's highest battlefield at 6,000m+. NLI soldiers maintain posts year-round in -60°C temperatures. More casualties from weather than enemy fire.

🏜

Cholistan Desert

XXXI Corps specializes in desert warfare across the Cholistan and Thar regions. Extreme heat, sand, and long supply lines define operations here.

🌊

Makran Coast

V Corps guards Pakistan's 1,046km coastline. Gwadar Port — the CPEC centrepiece — is heavily guarded against maritime threats and BLA sabotage.

🏔

Hindu Kush / KPK

XI Corps operates in the most rugged counter-insurgency terrain on earth. The tribal mountains of former FATA remain the army's most contested operational zone.

🌾

Punjab Plains

The heartland of conventional armoured warfare. Multiple Corps guard the Eastern border — the primary front against India — across the flat plains suited to tank battles.

🛣

Karakoram Highway

The KKH — the world's highest paved international road — connects Pakistan to China. Army engineers maintain and guard this critical artery through Gilgit-Baltistan.

🏗

Balochistan Plateau

XII Corps covers Pakistan's largest province — 44% of land area. Operations against BLA, BLF, and CPEC protection define the mission in this resource-rich but restive region.

🏙

Urban Centres

The army maintains rapid reaction capability for urban operations in Karachi, Lahore, and Islamabad — the three megacities that form Pakistan's economic and political heart.

Nation's First Responder

Disaster Relief — Civil-Military Operations

In a country that faces earthquakes, floods, and avalanches routinely, the Pakistan Army is the first and often only institution capable of rapid large-scale response.

2005
Kashmir Earthquake

7.6 magnitude — 87,000 killed, 3.5 million homeless. Army deployed 100,000 troops, engineers, and medics. Coordinated international relief via army logistics networks. Helicopter operations in cut-off mountain villages saved thousands from winter exposure.

⬡ 100,000 Troops Deployed
2010
Super Floods

Worst floods in Pakistan's recorded history. One-fifth of the country inundated. 20 million affected. Army deployed 170,000 troops, rescued 500,000+ civilians, built 278 emergency bridges, and restored 8,000km of roads during and after the disaster.

⬡ 170,000 Troops — Largest Relief Operation
2012
Gayari Avalanche

138 soldiers buried alive at Gayari post on the Siachen Glacier — the single worst disaster in Pakistan Army history. A rescue operation lasting weeks in extreme altitude and cold conditions recovered bodies from under 25 metres of ice and snow.

⬡ 138 Soldiers Lost — Worst Single Disaster
2015
Chitral Floods

Flash floods and landslides cut off Chitral district entirely. Army helicopters conducted 1,200+ sorties to deliver relief supplies and evacuate the sick and injured. Army engineers rebuilt the destroyed Lowari Tunnel access road in record time.

⬡ 1,200+ Helicopter Sorties
2022
Catastrophic Floods

One-third of Pakistan submerged. 33 million affected, 1,700 killed. Army deployed 177,000 troops, 50+ helicopters, and 1,200 boats — rescuing over 900,000 stranded civilians in the largest humanitarian operation in Pakistan's history.

⬡ 177,000 Troops — 900,000 Rescued
2023–26
Ongoing NDMA Support

The army maintains permanent National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA) liaison teams, pre-positioned engineering units, and rapid reaction aviation. In early 2026, army responded to flash floods in KPK and GB within 24 hours of disaster onset.

⬡ Year-Round NDMA Integration
2020s & Beyond

Modernisation — Army 2026

The Pakistan Army of 2026 is a transformed force — nuclear-capable, digitally networked, and simultaneously managing conventional deterrence and active counter-insurgency.

Transformation Since 2010

The counter-insurgency crucible of the 2000s and 2010s fundamentally reshaped the Pakistan Army. A force built for conventional warfare against India had to adapt — rapidly — to fight a non-state insurgency in its own backyard. The lessons learned in Swat, Waziristan, and the tribal belt created a more versatile, battle-hardened army.

By 2026, the army operates across six simultaneous mission sets: conventional deterrence (India), nuclear deterrence, counter-insurgency (KPK/Balochistan), CPEC security, UN peacekeeping, and domestic disaster relief — a breadth unmatched by armies of similar size.

  • Inducted Al-Khalid II Main Battle Tank — Pakistan's most advanced domestic tank platform
  • AH-1Z Viper attack helicopters replacing aging Cobras in Army Aviation
  • Chinese-supplied Wing Loong and CASC Rainbow UAVs operational
  • 2,600km+ Afghanistan border fence completed (2021–2024)
  • Nationwide National Command Authority communication network upgrade
  • CPEC Special Security Division expanded to 15,000+ personnel
  • Night vision, body armour, and modern small arms standardised across all units
  • Cyber warfare and electronic intelligence capabilities being established
🛡

Nuclear Deterrence

Pakistan maintains a credible second-strike capability with land-based Shaheen series missiles, short-range Nasr tactical nuclear weapons, and a developing sea-based deterrent via the Babur-3 SLCM. Doctrine remains "minimum credible deterrence."

🚁

Drone Warfare

The army has integrated armed drones extensively since 2015. Pakistani-made Burraq and Shahpar drones have conducted hundreds of strikes in tribal areas. Chinese WZ-10 armed drones added attack helicopter-equivalent capability.

🛰

Surveillance & ISR

Integration of satellite imagery, drone ISR feeds, and signals intelligence has transformed targeting precision. Intelligence-based operations now account for the majority of counter-terrorism action — dramatically reducing collateral damage versus conventional sweeps.

🌐

CPEC Security Architecture

A dedicated digital command and control network links SSD battalions along CPEC routes. Thermal imaging towers, reaction force bases every 50km, and joint civil-military coordination centres protect Pakistan's most important economic infrastructure.
